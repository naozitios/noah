# Investing is About Following Where Money Wants to Go

*I don’t profess to be a guru - I am still refining my investment frameworks as I go along.*

I have read a ton of books, threads, and YouTube videos on investing, valuation, accounting, and narratives. But all of these in isolation don't tell me the principle behind making money in the markets, which is: **what makes capital move from one market to another?**

After spending the last few years building products, studying businesses, modelling companies, and speaking to investors across different strategies, I keep coming back to the same conclusion.

It's about understanding ***where capital will flow next.*** That sounds suspiciously close to "buy low, sell high", but I think it is a much more useful framing.

Money doesn't randomly appear somewhere. It tends to move because something changes, and the job of an investor is figuring out ***what changes before the crowd notices.***

---

# Content

This is my current mental model for thinking about investments. It isn't a checklist for buying stocks; it's a framework for understanding why prices move.

Over time, I expect parts of this to change, but the broad ideas have remained surprisingly consistent.

The framework has three parts:

1. Investing is about mispricing
2. Every investment has a cost of capital
3. Prices move when capital changes direction

# Investing Is About Mispricing

The simplest definition of investing is this:

> **You make money when reality differs from what the market currently believes.**

If everyone already knows something, there is rarely an edge left. This is why sometimes when earnings calls report insane quarterly growth, the underlying stock price doesn't move (and sometimes even drops).

The opportunity comes from finding something that the market has either:

- misunderstood,
- underestimated,
- ignored,
- or simply hasn't looked at yet.

That sounds obvious, but in practice it is incredibly difficult because markets are rarely wrong for obvious reasons.

They're wrong because information takes time to spread, people anchor to old narratives, institutions move slowly, or research coverage is uneven. Oftentimes, people simply aren't paying attention.

# Types of Mispricing

I intentionally avoid focusing on quantitative mispricing, as it quickly becomes a discussion around ***statistical arbitrage, factor models, and machine learning.*** While technical indicators such as moving averages, Bollinger Bands, and support/resistance levels can be useful confirmation tools, I rarely use them as standalone signals. For longer-term investing, they should support—not replace—a fundamental thesis.

The two forms of mispricing I care more about are **fundamental** and **narrative**. *Fundamental improvements create value*, while narratives determine **when the market recognizes it**.

The biggest winners often have both: improving business fundamentals alongside expanding investor attention, leading to earnings upgrades and multiple rerating. Much of the AI rally—and, for periods, parts of the nuclear and quantum themes—followed this pattern.

---

### Fundamental Mispricing

Fundamental mispricing occurs when the market's expectations for a business differ from its future economic reality.

The primary way I test for this is by building a financial model. Once you've formed a view on revenue, margins, capital allocation, and long-term cash flows, you can estimate intrinsic value. If that value differs meaningfully from the market price, you've identified a potential fundamental mispricing.

Importantly, having a differentiated view doesn't necessarily mean you'll arrive at a different valuation. You can disagree with consensus on industry trends, interest rates, competitive moats, or growth drivers and still end up with a similar fair value. Ultimately, the investment thesis has to translate into different cash flows or a different valuation multiple.

It's easy to hear a compelling story—*"Rigetti will dominate quantum computing, so the stock should go up."* A more disciplined approach is to ask: *What does that actually imply for revenue, margins, and earnings?*

Build the model, estimate the cash flows or appropriate trading multiple, and then compare the implied valuation against today's price. That's where conviction comes from.

### Narrative Mispricing

Narrative mispricing occurs when the market has yet to fully recognize the significance of improving fundamentals. As attention shifts—through earnings, industry developments, or broader themes—investors begin to re-rate the company, often leading to multiple expansion.

Unlike fundamental analysis, narrative investing is about understanding where investor attention is moving.

Today's AI market is a good example. Early enthusiasm was concentrated around the chip layer; more recently, attention has expanded to infrastructure, software, and application companies as investors search for the next beneficiaries.

Narrative alone can generate exceptional returns, but it is also the most volatile source of alpha. When a thesis is driven primarily by sentiment, capital can rotate just as quickly as it arrived.

The strongest investments tend to combine both: improving fundamentals that justify higher earnings and a narrative that encourages the market to recognize them through a higher valuation multiple.

At least for now, since I haven’t found a reliable way to stay ahead of the narrative curve, my strategy is as follows:

Combining the two strategies is important, especially since some fundamentals eventually lead to narratives. Breaking it down:

1. What is the mispricing?
2. What needs to happen for everyone to reach that conclusion? (also known as signposting)

# Investing Is Both Science and Art

This is probably the biggest lesson I've learned.

The science is understanding the business and being able to forecast it with relatively decent accuracy.

- What are the key drivers?
- How will they change over time?
- What are its liquidity constraints and moats?
- Can it grow revenue at a consistent pace over time?

The art is understanding people and being able to answer questions like:

- When does attention shift?
- When do institutions start buying?
- What narrative becomes dominant?
- What causes multiples to rerate?
- What makes the market care?

What I learned is that a technically brilliant analysis can still produce poor returns if nobody is willing to reprice the asset.

Likewise, a weak business can generate extraordinary returns if capital temporarily flows toward the narrative.

The best investments tend to have both.

Strong improving fundamentals **and** a reason for the market to eventually recognize them.

## Now what?

I am continuing to refine my strategies for identifying these opportunities, but these are some strategies that I have discovered so far.

### Fundamental Mispricing Opportunities

Fundamental pricing is a function of deep knowledge in a field and the ability to create a financial model for the business in question. We can break this down into two parts:

**Deep industry knowledge**

- Interaction with the business and its competitors
    
    - Being a ***super*** customer often tells you everything you need to know about a business. By "super customer", I mean understanding both the problems you face and the problems other customers face.

- Understanding its financing
    
    - A company can serve its customers really well, but can it keep its doors open at its current pace? For example, OpenAI has built an incredible product, but the million-dollar question is whether it can sustain its capital requirements given its cash burn.

- Understanding its key drivers

### Narrative Mispricing Opportunities

**Understand the prevailing narrative**

Pretty much anything you see on public news, Reddit, and X is likely part of the prevailing narrative.

**Deconstruct the underlying assumptions**

- Look for assumptions that are fundamentally wrong
    
    These are often the easiest opportunities to identify. They are like bubbles waiting to burst—but the challenge is figuring out when they burst.

    An example is the explosion in market prices of quantum research stocks that had little to no revenue. Sure, they may have future value, but what exactly is being priced in today?

- Look for assumptions that have not yet been discovered
    
    An example of this is the large amount of capital flowing into chip and AI stocks. Perhaps, as an AI researcher, you understood that we would face an energy shortage or data center shortage, so you invest in areas where the broader narrative has not yet reached.

    A great amount of second-order thinking helps in this area.

**Monitor assumption changes**

Signposting means listing out what needs to happen for the narrative to change. This prevents you from staying exposed to a theme for too long and tying your capital to something that may never happen.

# Criticisms

### Who are you to give me investment advice?

You are absolutely right, I am not giving investment advice. I am simply sharing my mental models around this space.

I’d love to hear opposing views and am always open to conversations. Feel free to reach out if you are interested 🙂

### Forecasting where a company is going is crystal balling - nobody can predict the future

That is true. You can't predict the specifics, but perhaps you can place bets within a reasonable range where there is still potential upside.

Saying AAPL revenue will grow 2-3x over the next 20 years gives you a different present value compared to assuming it grows only 1.5-2x over the same period.

### The market can stay wrong

That is true, and that is why you need to track narratives to understand when—or if—the market will eventually recognize the true value of the business.
