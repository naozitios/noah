# Shorting the Volatility Index (VIX)

_April 10, 2025_

While much of the market is focused on predicting the next tariff headline, I believe the more important opportunity lies elsewhere.

Markets eventually adapt to uncertainty. Once investors become better at pricing political risk and tariff developments, volatility itself becomes the asset worth betting against.

Rather than trying to predict every policy announcement, I'd rather invest in something much simpler:

> **The eventual return of investor confidence.**

---

# Investment Thesis

> **Position:** Short the VIX during periods of extreme volatility while actively monitoring macro developments.

My view is built around a simple observation.

Periods of panic rarely last forever.

The VIX measures expected market volatility through S&P 500 option pricing. During crises, option premiums expand rapidly as investors rush to hedge downside risk. As uncertainty fades, those premiums typically compress and the VIX returns toward its long-term average.

The current tariff-driven environment appears to fit that pattern.

---

# The VIX Has Reached Extreme Levels

Recent tariff announcements have created significant uncertainty across global markets.

At the time of writing:

- VIX peaked at **60.1**
- Currently trading around **48.0**
- More than **2×** its 2025 average of **20.9**

Historically, spikes of this magnitude have been rare.

Two recent examples stand out:

| Event                              | Peak Volatility | Outcome                           |
| ---------------------------------- | --------------: | --------------------------------- |
| COVID-19 Market Shock (March 2020) |  Extremely High | Mean reverted over several months |
| August 2024 Jobs Report Sell-off   |        Elevated | Mean reverted within weeks        |

In both cases, volatility eventually normalized as uncertainty declined.

The current environment shares several similarities.

Investor sentiment remains deeply pessimistic:

- CNN Fear & Greed Index sits in **Extreme Fear**
- Consumer sentiment has fallen toward levels previously seen during:
  - the 2008 Financial Crisis
  - the 2001 Dot-com Bubble

When fear reaches extremes, volatility often becomes expensive.

---

# Tariff Headlines Are Driving Sentiment

One challenge with trading volatility today is that market direction depends heavily on unpredictable political developments.

Because of this, forecasting short-term price movements using historical models becomes increasingly difficult.

Instead, I prefer thinking in terms of scenarios.

Recent developments already demonstrate how quickly sentiment can shift.

The announcement of a **90-day tariff pause** immediately reduced fears of an escalating trade conflict.

The market responded accordingly.

The VIX declined roughly **20%** from its recent highs as investors became more optimistic that the revised tariff policies would be less damaging than initially feared.

Apple also emerged as one of the primary beneficiaries after smartphones and computers were excluded from portions of the tariff list.

Developments like these gradually restore confidence and reduce demand for expensive downside protection.

---

# Technical Perspective

Although macro headlines dominate short-term market direction, technical indicators can still provide useful context.

One indicator I monitor is the **28-day Relative Strength Index (RSI-28).**

Historically, when RSI rises above **70**, the VIX has often entered overbought territory.

Previous examples include:

- March 2020 COVID panic
- August 2024 jobs report shock

In both instances:

- volatility expanded rapidly
- fear reached extreme levels
- the VIX eventually reverted toward its long-term average

Interestingly:

- During COVID, the VIX traded roughly **4×** its five-year average (~22.1) before gradually normalizing over roughly four months.
- During the August 2024 sell-off, it reached approximately **3×** its average before reverting within just a few weeks.

While every crisis is different, history suggests that extreme volatility tends to be temporary rather than permanent.

---

# Risks

The largest risk to this thesis is straightforward.

Tariff uncertainty may not be over.

President Trump's continued focus on reducing the U.S. trade deficit means additional tariff announcements remain possible, particularly as the 90-day pause approaches expiration.

Unexpected policy changes could trigger:

- another surge in market uncertainty
- renewed demand for options
- another spike in the VIX

Because of this, timing matters.

Being fundamentally correct while entering too early can still produce significant short-term losses.

---

# Putting It Together

The short-term direction of the VIX remains difficult to predict because it depends heavily on political news flow.

However, several factors support a medium-term short position:

- The VIX is trading significantly above its historical average.
- Investor sentiment remains near extreme pessimism.
- Markets have historically adapted to periods of uncertainty.
- Volatility tends to mean revert once fear begins fading.

Rather than attempting to forecast every tariff announcement, I'd rather position for the eventual normalization of market expectations.

---

# How I'm Positioning

My strategy is relatively simple.

I would maintain a **short VIX position** while closely monitoring tariff developments.

If new tariff announcements materially increase uncertainty, I would expect additional spikes in volatility and would consider gradually increasing my short exposure rather than exiting the position.

The thesis depends less on predicting the exact peak in volatility and more on the belief that today's uncertainty is unlikely to remain elevated indefinitely.

As markets digest new information and confidence gradually returns, I expect the VIX to trend back toward its long-term average.

---

# Final Thoughts

Volatility itself is rarely a long-term trend.

It is usually a temporary reflection of uncertainty.

Today's tariff-driven market has produced elevated option premiums and historically high readings in the VIX. While additional negative headlines could create further spikes, history suggests that extreme volatility eventually fades as investors gain clarity.

For that reason, I believe the more compelling investment isn't trying to predict the next headline—it's positioning for the market's eventual return to normalcy.

> **The best time to short fear is often when everyone else is paying the highest price for it.**
